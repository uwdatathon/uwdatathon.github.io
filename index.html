Data Insights For Optimizing Resource Allocation In Border and Custom Security

Carolyn Chen, Jove Pendapotan, Kevin Wijaya, Reuben Santoso, Samuel Purnama

Executive Summary

Our analysis of border crossings reveals higher non-commercial traffic at the US-Mexico border and significant seasonal peaks at the US-Canada border. Commercial crossings have surged at the US-Mexico border since 2019, surpassing US-Canada levels. We recommend increasing resources and infrastructure at the Mexico border, especially in Texas and California, and boosting personnel during peak months at the Canada border. By focusing on these high-traffic areas and using data-driven metrics, CBP can optimize resource allocation, improve security, and support growing trade and travel demands. Effectively optimizing trade facilitation, bolster security measures, and retaining international cooperation between Canada and Mexico.

To begin, we started by mapping an overview of the active checkpoints throughout the United States surrounding the Canada and Mexico borders with a monthly average of over 2000 crossings. With this vast amount of information, we decided to narrow our focus by categorizing most crossings into two different categories to find out what process and where needed to be optimized.

Commercial & Non-Commercial Crossings

The two main categories we discovered were commercial and non-commercial crossings with definitions as follows:

Non-Commercial Crossings : Pedestrians, Cars, Buses, and Trains
Commercial Crossings : Trucks (Loaded/Unloaded) and Rails

These definitions are revolved around the idea that these two categories requires different functions and tools to pass through border immigration and inspection. In addition, these two category of vehicles have different ‘reasons of intent’ to enter the country, thus would need different cases for handling

With that we constructed multiple visualizations to display average crosses during certain months and change in frequency of crossing over time.

In the commercial setting, the US-Canada border initially had a higher average number of crossings. However, starting around 2019, the US-Mexico border experienced a noticeable rise in frequency, eventually surpassing Canada in the average number of crossings each month. These indications showcase Mexico has a higher metric of change in people entering the country in recent years over Canada.

Meanwhile, in the non-commercial setting, the average non-commercial crossings per month in the US-Mexico borders consistently outnumbers the US-Canada borders. Yet, the US-Canada borders increases 60% to its peak during July to August every year. This likely could indicate a rise in tourism within the US-Canada around the summer season. Furthermore, even in this sector the US-Mexico border shows a strong growth in crosses.

Thus, with overall rise in activity from the US-Mexico border annually, and a small spikes during summer times for the Canadian border, the CBP should:

1. Allocate more resources towards the borders with Mexico
2. Faciliate Mexico with more heavy duty equipment for industry screening
3. Rotate resources to Canada between July-August

A viable option could be to temporary hire security in Canada during peak months, but a regular change in employment may pose a security risk.

Commercial Vehicle Crossings in Texas: 1996-2023

From 1996 to 2023, commercial vehicle crossings in Texas consistently increased, with accelerated growth starting around 2010. The crossings grew approximately 260%, from 4.5 million to 11.8 million. Initially, the cumulative commercial crossings in Michigan and New York (bordering Toronto) were higher than in Texas, but Texas's growth has since widened the gap. This indicates the shift of industrial development favoring Mexico.

Texas's significant growth in commercial crossings, particularly after 2010, is likely due to increased industrial activity and its strategic location near Mexico. Major crossings like El Paso handle substantial industrial traffic, supporting supply chains in sectors such as automotive, electronics, and manufacturing. Texas serves as a crucial link in the US-Mexico industrial commodity flow. To anticipate future industrial growth, it is recommended to allocate more resources or heavy-duty security to tackle the busy crossings on the border of Texas. Other recommendations?

Non-Commercial Vehicle Crossings in Texas: 1996-2023

With our visualizations from 1996 to 2023, Texas consistently led in non-commercial crossings among the top three busiest states, experiencing significant growth until the early 2000s. While, California gradually closed the gap with Texas, especially after 2010. This growth metric could reflect the growing opportunities or recreational appeal California offers. Hence, It would be recommended to allocate resources to tackle the possible future increase of crossings on the Mexico-California, especially in the San Ysidro or Otay Mesa ports as the two most busy ports in the California border.

Geographical Evaluation

The analysis highlights significant traffic volumes at the US-Mexico border, particularly in Texas and California, driven by both personal and commercial vehicles. This underscores the importance of robust infrastructure and security measures in these high-traffic areas. Similarly, the US-Canada border, especially at crossings in New York and Michigan, experiences considerable non-commercial traffic, suggesting a need for enhanced facilities and streamlined processes to accommodate personal vehicle and pedestrian crossings.

By leveraging these insights the U.S. Customs and Border Protection could concentrate their resources in putting more emphasis on these the states: California and Texas for the US-Mexico Border and New York and Michigan for the US-Canada Border. Our reasoning is justified through these two metrics, frequency of crossing and the change in total crossing over time. These metrics have allowed us to both prioritize key ports crucial to immigration and identify growing access points that requires support from commercial and non-commercial traffic.

Seasonal variations and weather conditions play a significant role in traffic patterns, revealing periods of peak activity and potential bottlenecks. This insight is vital for developing adaptive border management strategies that can respond to fluctuating demand and ensure efficient operations year-round.

Furthermore, the data shows a gradual increase in commercial traffic, reflecting broader economic trends and the growing importance of cross-border trade. This trend necessitates a forward-looking approach to infrastructure development, incorporating advanced technologies and predictive analytics to anticipate and manage future growth.

Weather Evaluation

Seasonal variations and weather conditions play a significant role in traffic patterns, revealing periods of peak activity and potential bottlenecks. This insight is vital for developing adaptive border management strategies. However, within our studies and analysis we found no strong relationship that highly suggest a weather or environmental factors influences the level of traffic throughout the year.

This shortcoming indicates the evaluation of resource management for border patrol should be independent from concern of weather or the environment. Re-highlighting the important of geographic factors into resource planning for both the US-Canada and US-Mexico control points. Higher security practices should be solely placed on rising crossing interest on a specific port and evaluative measures should be taken from there.
